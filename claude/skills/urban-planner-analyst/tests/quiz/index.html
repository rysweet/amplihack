    Urban Planner Analyst - Domain Validation Quiz¶

    Purpose¶

    This quiz validates that the urban planner analyst applies urban planning principles correctly, identifies spatial patterns and infrastructure needs, and provides evidence-based city development analysis. Each scenario requires demonstration of planning frameworks, land use analysis, and sustainable development reasoning.

    Scenario 1: Transit-Oriented Development Proposal¶

    Event Description: A mid-sized city (population 450,000) plans a new light rail transit (LRT) line connecting the downtown core to a suburban employment hub 12 miles away, with 15 stations. The city proposes rezoning areas within ½ mile of stations from single-family residential to mixed-use, allowing buildings up to 8 stories. The plan anticipates 8,500 new housing units and 12,000 jobs over 15 years. Current residents near proposed stations express concerns about traffic, parking, neighborhood character changes, and gentrification displacing long-term residents. The project requires $2.1 billion in infrastructure investment. Ridership projections estimate 45,000 daily riders by year 10.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the transit-oriented development proposal and its urban planning implications.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Transit-Oriented Development (TOD) Principles:
    • High-density, mixed-use development near transit stations
    • Walkable, pedestrian-oriented design (½ mile = 10-minute walk)
    • Reduced auto dependency and parking requirements
    • Integration of housing, employment, retail, services

    • Support for transit ridership through proximate development

    • Land Use and Zoning Analysis:

    • Current zoning: Single-family residential (low density)
    • Proposed zoning: Mixed-use, 8-story buildings (medium-high density)
    • Density increase: 8,500 housing units (estimate 17,000-21,000 residents)
    • Job-housing balance: 12,000 jobs + residential (reduces commute distances)
    • Floor Area Ratio (FAR) implications

    • Transition zones and height step-downs

    • Transportation Impact Assessment:

    • Mode shift: Projected auto trips reduced by transit use
    • Traffic: Short-term construction disruption, long-term reduction near stations
    • Parking: Reduced requirements (TOD parking ratios lower than suburban)
    • Bicycle and pedestrian infrastructure needs
    • First/last mile connectivity (bus feeders, bike share)

    • Regional congestion mitigation

    • Economic and Fiscal Analysis:

    • Capital cost: $2.1 billion (federal, state, local funding mix)
    • Operating costs: Annual LRT operations and maintenance
    • Property value impacts: Premiums near transit (10-20% typical)
    • Tax revenue: Increased assessed values, development fees
    • Cost-benefit ratio and economic development multipliers

    • Affordability and displacement risks

    • Equity and Gentrification Concerns:

    • Displacement risk: Long-term residents priced out by property value increases
    • Affordability requirements: Inclusionary zoning, affordable housing mandates
    • Anti-displacement strategies: Rent stabilization, community land trusts, tenant protections
    • Equitable development: Benefits distribution, community engagement

    • Environmental justice: Access to opportunity for low-income residents

    • Community Engagement and Opposition:

    • Stakeholder concerns: Traffic, parking, character, displacement
    • NIMBY dynamics: Local opposition to density and change
    • Participatory planning: Community input, co-design processes
    • Addressing concerns: Traffic calming, parking management, design guidelines

    • Building coalitions: Housing advocates, transit users, climate activists

    • Sustainability and Climate Goals:

    • VMT reduction: Vehicle miles traveled decrease (climate benefit)
    • Greenhouse gas emissions: Transportation sector reductions
    • Compact development: Efficient land use, reduced sprawl
    • Green building standards: LEED, net-zero energy

    • Urban heat island mitigation: Green infrastructure, tree canopy

    • Historical Context:

    • Portland, OR: Pioneer in TOD, streetcar and MAX light rail integration
    • Denver RTD FasTracks: Regional rail expansion with TOD zones
    • Arlington, VA Rosslyn-Ballston Corridor: TOD transformation from auto-oriented
    • Minneapolis Hiawatha Line: Gentrification and displacement concerns
    • Research: Transit proximity premiums, ridership-development feedback loops

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of TOD principles, land use planning
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of transportation, economic, equity analysis
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear policy recommendations, specific anti-displacement measures
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to TOD precedents, research evidence
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from planning goals to implementation to outcomes

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 2: Climate Adaptation Planning¶

    Event Description: A coastal city (population 280,000) faces increasing climate risks: sea level rise projections of 2-3 feet by 2050, intensified hurricanes, and chronic "sunny day" flooding in low-lying neighborhoods. A vulnerability assessment identifies 12,000 residents in flood-prone areas, $4.5 billion in at-risk property, and critical infrastructure (wastewater treatment plant, power substations, hospital) in hazard zones. The city must balance protecting existing development, managing retreat from high-risk areas, and accommodating 15% population growth over 30 years. Funding constraints limit large-scale infrastructure (seawalls cost $10-20 million per mile). Socioeconomic disparities mean low-income communities face highest risk but least capacity to relocate.

    Analysis Task: Develop a climate adaptation strategy for the coastal city.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Climate Risk Assessment:
    • Sea level rise: 2-3 feet by 2050 (exponential thereafter)
    • Storm surge: Hurricane intensification, increased flood heights
    • Chronic flooding: King tides, "sunny day" nuisance flooding
    • Exposure: 12,000 residents, $4.5B property, critical infrastructure

    • Timeframe: Near-term (2030), mid-century (2050), long-term (2100)

    • Adaptation Strategy Framework:

    • Protect: Seawalls, levees, floodwalls (high cost, finite protection)
    • Accommodate: Flood-resistant design, elevated structures, wet-proof buildings
    • Retreat: Managed relocation from highest-risk areas
    • Hybrid approach: Risk-based strategies by area

    • Nature-based solutions: Living shorelines, wetland restoration

    • Land Use and Zoning Strategies:

    • Floodplain regulations: Prohibit new development in high-risk zones
    • Overlay districts: Special standards for coastal areas
    • Transfer of development rights (TDR): Incentivize relocation to safer areas
    • Density concentration: Direct growth to inland, higher-elevation areas

    • Rolling easements: Allow natural shoreline migration

    • Infrastructure Resilience:

    • Critical facilities: Relocate or harden (hospital, wastewater plant, power)
    • Transportation: Elevate roads, design for temporary flooding
    • Stormwater management: Green infrastructure, pump stations
    • Utilities: Underground vs. overhead, redundancy

    • Emergency access: Evacuation routes, shelter capacity

    • Managed Retreat and Relocation:

    • Buyout programs: Purchase high-risk properties at fair market value
    • Relocation assistance: Moving costs, down payment support
    • Land banking: Convert bought-out areas to open space, parks
    • Voluntary vs. mandatory programs
    • Destination areas: Affordable housing in safer locations

    • Equity: Ensure low-income residents can afford relocation

    • Equity and Environmental Justice:

    • Disproportionate impacts: Low-income and minority communities in flood zones
    • Capacity to adapt: Financial resources, political influence
    • Affordable housing preservation: Prevent climate gentrification
    • Community engagement: Affected residents in decision-making

    • Just transition: Support for displaced businesses and residents

    • Financing and Implementation:

    • Federal programs: FEMA BRIC, HUD CDBG-DR, Army Corps projects
    • State and local funding: Bonds, resilience taxes, stormwater fees
    • Cost-benefit analysis: Avoided damages vs. adaptation costs
    • Phased implementation: Prioritize highest-risk, critical infrastructure

    • Timeline: 30-year horizon with 5-year action plans

    • Historical Context:

    • New Orleans post-Katrina: Levees, buyouts, population loss
    • Miami Beach: Raised roads, pump stations, "sunny day" flooding
    • NYC post-Sandy: Resilience plans, Build It Back program
    • Staten Island buyouts: Managed retreat from flood-prone areas
    • Norfolk, VA: Recurrent flooding, adaptation planning
    • Research: Climate migration, adaptation limits, maladaptation risks

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of adaptation planning, risk assessment frameworks
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of strategy options, equity considerations, financing
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear prioritization, specific relocation and infrastructure measures
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to adaptation precedents, climate resilience research
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from risk assessment to strategy selection to implementation

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 3: Affordable Housing Crisis¶

    Event Description: A rapidly growing tech hub city (population 650,000) faces severe housing affordability challenges. Median home price is $875,000 (up 65% in 5 years), median rent is $2,400/month (up 45%). Cost-burdened households (>30% income on housing) comprise 52% of renters and 35% of owners. Homelessness has increased 38% in 3 years to 4,200 individuals. The jobs-housing imbalance worsens as tech employment grows 8% annually but housing production lags (3,500 units/year vs. 8,000 needed). Long commutes from distant affordable areas strain transportation systems and increase emissions. Neighborhood resistance to new development (especially multi-family) persists despite the crisis.

    Analysis Task: Develop a comprehensive affordable housing strategy.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Housing Affordability Assessment:
    • Affordability standard: 30% of income on housing
    • Cost burden: 52% renters, 35% owners exceed threshold
    • Affordability gap: $875K home requires $200K+ income (at 30% threshold)
    • Rental affordability: $2,400/mo requires $96K income
    • Supply-demand imbalance: 8,000 units needed vs. 3,500 produced

    • Job growth outpacing housing: 8% employment vs. housing lag

    • Root Cause Analysis:

    • Supply constraints: Zoning restrictions, development barriers
    • Land use: Single-family zoning limits density (70-80% of residential land typical)
    • Construction costs: Labor, materials, land prices
    • Regulatory barriers: Permitting delays, impact fees, parking requirements
    • NIMBY opposition: Neighborhood resistance to density

    • Speculation and investment: Housing as commodity vs. shelter

    • Zoning and Land Use Reforms:

    • Upzoning: Allow higher density, multi-family in single-family zones
    • Missing middle housing: Duplexes, triplexes, townhomes, courtyard apartments
    • Density bonuses: Extra height/density for affordable units
    • Eliminate exclusionary zoning: Minimum lot sizes, parking mandates
    • Accessory Dwelling Units (ADUs): Legalize and streamline

    • Transit-oriented development: High density near transit

    • Affordable Housing Production:

    • Inclusionary zoning: Require percentage of affordable units in new development
    • Public housing: Direct government construction and management
    • Social housing: Non-profit, cooperative, community land trust models
    • Subsidies: Low-Income Housing Tax Credits (LIHTC), project-based vouchers
    • Density bonus programs: Developer incentives for affordability

    • Target: 30% of new units at 60-80% AMI, 10% at 30-50% AMI

    • Anti-Displacement and Preservation:

    • Rent stabilization: Limit rent increases in existing units
    • Tenant protections: Just cause eviction, relocation assistance
    • Acquisition and preservation: Purchase at-risk affordable housing
    • Community land trusts: Permanent affordability through ground leases

    • Affordable housing overlay zones: Protect existing affordable areas

    • Addressing Homelessness:

    • Housing First approach: Rapid rehousing without preconditions
    • Supportive housing: Permanent housing + services for chronically homeless
    • Emergency shelter: Immediate capacity expansion
    • Prevention: Rental assistance, eviction prevention programs
    • Coordinated entry: Centralized assessment and resource allocation

    • Scale: 4,200 individuals need pathways to housing

    • Financing and Revenue:

    • Affordable housing bonds: Voter-approved debt for production
    • Linkage fees: Commercial development fees for housing fund
    • Real estate transfer taxes: Revenue from property sales
    • Land value capture: Tax increment financing in upzoned areas
    • Federal and state funding: HOME, CDBG, state housing trust funds

    • Public land: Surplus public sites for affordable housing

    • Regional Coordination:

    • Jobs-housing balance: Employment centers provide or fund housing
    • Regional Housing Needs Assessment (RHNA): Allocation of growth targets
    • Fair share: Suburban jurisdictions accept affordable housing

    • Transportation-housing coordination: Transit investments with housing

    • Historical Context:

    • Vienna social housing: 60% of residents in public/social housing
    • Minneapolis zoning reform (2018): Legalized triplexes citywide
    • Oregon statewide duplex law (2019): Mandate middle housing
    • California SB 9 and 10: State-level zoning reforms
    • Seattle HALA (2015): Upzoning and affordable housing production
    • Research: Filtering theory, supply elasticity, inclusionary zoning effectiveness

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of housing policy, zoning reform principles
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of root cause, production strategy, anti-displacement
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear policy recommendations, specific zoning and financing measures
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to housing policy precedents, research evidence
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from crisis to causes to solutions

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 4: Post-Industrial Neighborhood Revitalization¶

    Event Description: A former manufacturing district (450 acres, 3,200 residents) has been economically depressed for 30 years following factory closures. Current conditions: 35% vacancy rate in commercial buildings, median household income 40% below city average, unemployment 12% (vs. 5% citywide), aging housing stock in disrepair, contaminated brownfield sites (12 parcels), limited retail and services, high crime rates, underperforming schools. The city has designated the area as a redevelopment zone with tax increment financing, streamlined permitting, and infrastructure investments planned. Private developers express interest in mixed-use projects, but residents fear gentrification and displacement. Community organizations demand local hiring, affordable housing, and preservation of neighborhood character.

    Analysis Task: Develop an equitable neighborhood revitalization plan.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Neighborhood Assessment:
    • Physical: 35% vacancy, brownfields, aging housing, infrastructure deficits
    • Economic: Low income (40% below city), 12% unemployment, limited retail
    • Social: High crime, underperforming schools, social isolation
    • Assets: Historic architecture, proximity to downtown, engaged community

    • Opportunity: Redevelopment potential, market interest, public investment

    • Revitalization Goals and Trade-offs:

    • Economic development vs. affordability
    • New investment vs. displacement of existing residents
    • Physical improvement vs. cultural preservation
    • Market-rate development vs. subsidized housing

    • Balancing competing interests: Developers, residents, city

    • Brownfield Remediation and Reuse:

    • Environmental assessment and cleanup (12 contaminated parcels)
    • Funding: EPA Brownfield Grants, state programs, developer contributions
    • Reuse planning: Mixed-use, parks, stormwater management
    • Liability protections: Voluntary cleanup programs, environmental covenants

    • Timeline and phasing: Cleanup precedes development

    • Land Use and Redevelopment Strategy:

    • Mixed-use zoning: Residential, commercial, light industrial, maker spaces
    • Adaptive reuse: Convert vacant industrial buildings to housing, retail, offices
    • Infill development: New construction on vacant parcels
    • Historic preservation: Retain character-defining features

    • Green infrastructure: Stormwater parks, green streets, urban agriculture

    • Affordable Housing and Anti-Displacement:

    • Inclusionary requirements: 25-30% affordable in new development
    • Existing housing rehabilitation: Preserve affordable units
    • Community land trusts: Permanent affordability mechanism
    • Tenant protections: Anti-displacement vouchers, relocation assistance

    • Target: No net loss of affordable units, increase affordable supply

    • Economic Development and Job Creation:

    • Local hiring mandates: Construction jobs (30-40%), permanent jobs (20-30%)
    • Workforce development: Training programs for neighborhood residents
    • Small business support: Technical assistance, low-interest loans, below-market rent
    • Retail and services: Grocery store, healthcare, childcare (reduce neighborhood services gap)

    • Living wage requirements: Ensure jobs lift families out of poverty

    • Community Engagement and Governance:

    • Participatory planning: Resident-led visioning and design
    • Community benefits agreement (CBA): Legally binding developer commitments
    • Ongoing oversight: Community advisory board, development review
    • Transparent decision-making: Public data, accessible meetings

    • Build trust: Address historical disinvestment and broken promises

    • Financing and Incentives:

    • Tax Increment Financing (TIF): Capture increased property tax revenue
    • Opportunity Zones: Federal tax incentives (monitor for equity outcomes)
    • New Markets Tax Credits (NMTC): Investment in low-income communities
    • Public infrastructure: Streets, utilities, parks (catalyze private investment)

    • Phasing: Public investment first, then private development

    • Historical Context:

    • Pittsburgh Strip District: Industrial to mixed-use, some displacement
    • Atlanta BeltLine: Rail-to-trail, TOD, gentrification concerns, CBA
    • Dudley Street Neighborhood Initiative (Boston): Community land trust, resident control
    • San Francisco Mission District: Gentrification resistance, anti-displacement measures
    • Buffalo waterfront revitalization: Brownfield cleanup, public access
    • Research: Gentrification patterns, anti-displacement strategies, TIF effectiveness

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of redevelopment planning, brownfield reuse
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of equity strategies, economic development, community engagement
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear anti-displacement measures, specific local hiring and affordability targets
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to revitalization precedents, equity research
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical balance of growth and equity, investment and protection

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 5: Regional Growth Management¶

    Event Description: A metropolitan region (1.8 million population, 5 counties, 42 municipalities) faces uncoordinated sprawl development. Over 20 years, population grew 35% but urbanized land increased 65% (low-density suburban expansion). Consequences include: VMT increased 48% (traffic congestion, emissions), agricultural land loss (45,000 acres converted), infrastructure costs ($8 billion in roads, water, sewer for dispersed development), school overcrowding in suburbs, job-housing imbalance (long commutes), and environmental impacts (habitat fragmentation, water quality degradation). The region's core cities experience population decline, vacant properties, and declining tax bases while suburbs resist affordable housing and transit.

    Analysis Task: Develop a regional growth management framework.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Sprawl Assessment and Impacts:
    • Sprawl pattern: 35% population growth, 65% land consumption (inefficient)
    • Transportation: 48% VMT increase, congestion, emissions
    • Fiscal: $8B infrastructure costs for dispersed development
    • Environmental: 45,000 acres farmland lost, habitat fragmentation, water quality
    • Social: Segregation by income and race, job-housing imbalance, long commutes

    • Equity: Suburban exclusion of affordable housing, core city disinvestment

    • Regional Governance Challenges:

    • Fragmentation: 42 municipalities, competing interests
    • Race to the bottom: Jurisdictions compete for tax base, resist affordable housing
    • Coordination failures: No mechanism for regional decisions
    • State vs. local: Home rule traditions, resistance to state mandates

    • Incentive misalignment: Municipalities benefit from growth, externalize costs

    • Growth Management Strategies:

    • Urban Growth Boundaries (UGBs): Contain urban expansion, direct infill
    • Regional plans: Coordinated land use, transportation, housing
    • Infill and redevelopment: Prioritize core cities and inner suburbs
    • Agricultural preservation: Purchase development rights, ag zoning
    • Green infrastructure: Regional trail networks, habitat corridors

    • Performance standards: Density minimums, VMT targets, affordable housing quotas

    • Regional Transportation-Land Use Integration:

    • Transit investments: Regional rail, bus rapid transit in dense corridors
    • TOD around stations: Coordinated zoning and housing targets
    • Complete streets: Walkable, bikeable neighborhoods
    • VMT reduction goals: 20-30% per capita by 2040
    • Congestion pricing: Disincentivize driving, fund transit

    • Parking policies: Reduce minimums, price on-street parking

    • Fair Share Affordable Housing:

    • Regional Housing Needs Assessment: Allocate housing targets by jurisdiction
    • Affordable housing mandates: All jurisdictions accept fair share
    • Enforcement mechanisms: State override of exclusionary zoning, funding penalties
    • Funding pooling: Regional housing trust fund, cross-subsidies

    • Target: 20-25% of regional housing at affordable levels

    • Infrastructure and Service Efficiency:

    • Compact development: Lower per-capita infrastructure costs
    • Shared services: Regional water, transit, waste management
    • Fiscal impact analysis: Full-cost accounting for sprawl vs. infill
    • Green infrastructure: Natural stormwater management vs. grey infrastructure

    • School planning: Coordinate land use and education capacity

    • Environmental Protection:

    • Critical habitat: No-development zones, conservation easements
    • Farmland preservation: Ag districts, differential tax assessment
    • Water quality: Source water protection, watershed management
    • Climate mitigation: Compact development reduces emissions

    • Ecosystem services: Maintain forests, wetlands, floodplains

    • Governance Models and Tools:

    • Metropolitan Planning Organization (MPO): Transportation and land use coordination
    • Regional councils: Voluntary coordination (weak) or binding authority (strong)
    • State mandates: Growth management requirements (OR, WA, MD models)
    • Tax-base sharing: Minnesota Fiscal Disparities model

    • Regulatory tools: State zoning overrides, regional permits

    • Historical Context:

    • Portland Metro: UGB, regional planning authority, transit-land use integration
    • Minneapolis-St. Paul: Fiscal disparities tax-base sharing (1971)
    • Oregon statewide land use planning (1973): UGBs, agricultural preservation
    • Maryland Smart Growth (1997): Priority Funding Areas, rural preservation
    • New Jersey State Plan: Cross-acceptance process, growth tiers
    • Research: Sprawl costs, compact development benefits, regional governance effectiveness

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of growth management, regional planning principles
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of sprawl impacts, governance strategies, implementation tools
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear regional coordination mechanisms, specific growth management policies
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to regional planning precedents, research evidence
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from sprawl diagnosis to regional coordination to implementation

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Passing Criteria¶

    To demonstrate urban planner analyst competence:

    • Minimum per scenario: 35/50 (70%)
    • Overall minimum: 175/250 (70%)
    • Must pass at least 4 of 5 scenarios

    Evaluation Dimensions¶

    Each scenario is scored on:

    1. Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of urban planning frameworks and principles
    2. Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness and sophistication of spatial and policy analysis
    3. Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear, actionable planning recommendations
    4. Historical Grounding (0-10): Use of precedents, case studies, planning research
    5. Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow, coherent development strategy

    What High-Quality Analysis Looks Like¶

    Excellent (45-50 points):

    • Applies planning frameworks accurately (comprehensive planning, zoning, TOD, climate adaptation)
    • Considers spatial, economic, social, environmental, and equity dimensions
    • Makes specific, prioritized policy recommendations with implementation pathways
    • Cites relevant planning precedents, research, and best practices
    • Clear logical flow from analysis to strategy to implementation
    • Acknowledges trade-offs and community engagement needs
    • Identifies innovative planning tools and approaches

    Good (35-44 points):

    • Applies key planning concepts correctly
    • Considers main land use, transportation, and equity factors
    • Makes reasonable policy recommendations
    • References some precedents or planning practices
    • Clear reasoning
    • Provides useful planning insights

    Needs Improvement (<35 points):

    • Misapplies planning concepts or frameworks
    • Ignores critical spatial patterns or equity concerns
    • Vague or technically incorrect recommendations
    • Lacks grounding in planning precedents or research
    • Unclear or illogical reasoning
    • Superficial spatial analysis

    Using This Quiz¶

    For Self-Assessment¶

    1. Attempt each scenario analysis
    2. Compare your analysis to expected elements
    3. Score yourself honestly on each dimension
    4. Identify areas for improvement

    For Automated Testing (Claude Agent SDK)¶

    from claude_agent_sdk import Agent, TestHarness

agent = Agent.load("urban-planner-analyst")
quiz = load_quiz_scenarios("tests/quiz.md")

results = []
for scenario in quiz.scenarios:
    analysis = agent.analyze(scenario.event)
    score = evaluate_analysis(analysis, scenario.expected_elements)
    results.append({"scenario": scenario.name, "score": score})

assert sum(r["score"] for r in results) >= 175  # Overall passing
assert sum(1 for r in results if r["score"] >= 35) >= 4  # At least 4 scenarios pass

    For Continuous Improvement¶

    • Add new scenarios as urban challenges evolve
    • Update expected elements as planning practices change
    • Refine scoring criteria based on analyst performance patterns
    • Use failures to improve urban planner analyst skill
