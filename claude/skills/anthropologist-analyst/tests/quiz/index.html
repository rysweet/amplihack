    Anthropologist Analyst - Domain Validation Quiz¶

    Purpose¶

    This quiz validates that the anthropologist analyst applies anthropological frameworks correctly, identifies cultural patterns and meanings, and provides well-grounded ethnographic analysis. Each scenario requires demonstration of anthropological reasoning, cultural theory application, and evidence-based interpretation.

    Scenario 1: Corporate Culture Clash in Multinational Merger¶

    Event Description: US tech company acquires Japanese manufacturing firm. Integration struggles emerge immediately: American executives push "flat hierarchy" and "speak up culture," but Japanese employees remain silent in meetings. Americans email directly to any level; Japanese insist on going through proper channels. Deadlines constantly missed - Americans interpret as incompetence, Japanese see American timelines as unrealistic. Gift-giving by Japanese team seen as bribery concern by US compliance. Japanese expect after-work socialization for team building; Americans leave at 5pm. Turnover in Japanese subsidiary reaches 30% in first year. Both sides frustrated, seeing the other as difficult and unprofessional.

    Analysis Task: Provide anthropological analysis of the cultural clash and recommendations.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Cultural Dimensions Theory (Hofstede):
    • Power Distance: US low (flat hierarchy), Japan high (respect for hierarchy)
    • Individualism vs. Collectivism: US individualist (speak up), Japan collectivist (group harmony)
    • Uncertainty Avoidance: Japan high (process, planning), US lower (flexibility)
    • Long-term vs. Short-term Orientation: Japan long-term, US quarterly focus

    • Masculinity/Femininity: Different approaches to work-life balance

    • High-Context vs. Low-Context Communication (Hall):

    • US: Low-context - direct, explicit, verbal communication
    • Japan: High-context - indirect, implicit, contextual cues
    • Silence as communication in Japanese culture (vs. discomfort in US)
    • Email directness vs. face-saving through intermediaries

    • Misinterpretation as incompetence rather than cultural difference

    • Face and Social Harmony (Goffman, East Asian concepts):

    • Face-work: Preserving dignity and reputation
    • Honne vs. Tatemae: Private feelings vs. public facade
    • Nemawashi: Pre-meeting consensus building (invisible to Americans)
    • Speaking up in meetings = potential face loss for Japanese

    • Direct criticism violates Japanese norms of indirection

    • Gift Exchange and Reciprocity (Mauss, Malinowski):

    • Gift-giving as relationship building (Japanese cultural practice)
    • Reciprocity obligations create social bonds
    • Kula ring analog: Gifts circulate, create networks
    • American interpretation through compliance/corruption lens

    • Cultural relativism: Gift vs. bribe depends on cultural framework

    • Time Orientation and Polychronic vs. Monochronic (Hall):

    • Monochronic (US): Linear time, one thing at a time, deadlines sacred
    • Polychronic (Japan in relationships): Flexible time for relationship maintenance
    • But: Japanese precision in manufacturing context
    • Missed deadlines = different time philosophies + process differences

    • After-work socializing = relationship building (not optional in Japanese frame)

    • Organizational Culture as Symbolic System (Geertz, Schein):

    • Thick description: Multiple layers of meaning in behaviors
    • Artifacts: Office layout, dress codes, meeting rituals
    • Espoused values vs. basic assumptions divergence
    • Integration challenge: Not just process, but worldviews

    • Sensemaking: Each culture interprets other through own framework

    • Emic vs. Etic Perspectives:

    • Emic (insider): How Japanese employees experience "silence," "hierarchy," "gifts"
    • Etic (outsider): American interpretation of same behaviors
    • Misattribution: Both sides assume bad intent rather than cultural logic
    • Anthropological relativism: Understand behavior in cultural context

    • Neither culture "right" - different adaptive strategies

    • Ethnographic Recommendations:

    • Cultural liaison roles: Bicultural brokers
    • Cross-cultural training: Both directions, not just Japanese adapting
    • Hybrid practices: Negotiated third culture
    • Participant observation: Leaders experience both contexts
    • Explicit negotiation of norms rather than assumption of universality

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of cultural dimensions, context theory, gift exchange
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of symbolic analysis, multiple frameworks
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear cultural mechanisms, specific recommendations
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to anthropological theory, cross-cultural research
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from observation to cultural interpretation

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 2: Traditional Healing vs. Biomedicine in Indigenous Community¶

    Event Description: Public health initiative introduces Western medical clinic in indigenous community in Amazon. After 2 years, utilization remains low (20% of population). Community continues visiting traditional healers for most ailments. Health workers frustrated, citing "ignorance" and "superstition." Ethnographic observation reveals: Illness attributed to spiritual causes (soul loss, spirit intrusion, sorcery), not just biological. Traditional healers provide explanatory frameworks connecting illness to social disruptions, dreams, and moral transgressions. Biomedicine seen as effective for "white people's diseases" but inadequate for "our diseases." Some community members use both systems, but hide traditional healing from clinic staff. Healers hold social prestige; doctors seen as outsiders.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the medical pluralism and cultural dimensions of health beliefs.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Medical Anthropology: Illness vs. Disease (Kleinman):
    • Disease: Biomedical category (pathophysiology)
    • Illness: Lived experience, cultural meaning
    • Sickness: Social dimension, legitimation
    • Traditional healing addresses illness and sickness, not just disease

    • Biomedical reductionism misses social and spiritual dimensions

    • Explanatory Models of Illness (Kleinman):

    • Each culture has folk models of etiology, symptoms, treatment
    • Indigenous model: Spiritual causation, social-relational
    • Biomedical model: Germ theory, mechanistic
    • Models are incommensurable, not hierarchical

    • Healers integrate illness into cultural worldview

    • Symbolic Healing and Ritual Efficacy (Levi-Strauss, Turner):

    • Healing rituals as symbolic systems providing meaning
    • Shamanic cure: Manipulation of symbols, narrative reconstruction
    • Efficacy through symbolic coherence, not biomedical mechanism
    • Ritual process: Separation, liminality, reintegration (Turner)

    • Placebo vs. culturally meaningful intervention

    • Medical Pluralism and Pragmatic Use:

    • Coexistence of multiple medical systems
    • Strategic use: Biomedicine for some conditions, traditional for others
    • "White people's diseases" = those brought by contact
    • "Our diseases" = culture-bound syndromes with spiritual etiology

    • Syncretism and compartmentalization

    • Social Organization and Authority:

    • Healers as cultural brokers, knowledge holders
    • Healing tied to social relationships and community standing
    • Biomedicine disrupts traditional authority structures
    • Trust based on shared cultural understanding and language

    • Doctors as outsiders lack social embeddedness

    • Structural Violence and Colonial Context (Farmer):

    • Historical trauma: Colonialism, forced assimilation, disease introduction
    • Biomedicine associated with colonizers
    • Structural inequalities in healthcare access
    • Traditional healing as cultural resistance

    • Power dynamics: Who defines "health" and "disease"?

    • Cultural Safety and Humility (Tervalon, Murray-Garcia):

    • Cultural imposition: Biomedicine as superior, universal
    • Cultural relativism: Each system valid within context
    • Critical medical anthropology: Acknowledge power but not uncritical acceptance of harmful practices
    • Partnership model: Integrate systems rather than replace

    • Community-driven health priorities

    • Applied Recommendations:

    • Ethnographic research: Understand local illness categories
    • Collaborate with traditional healers: Referral networks
    • Training: Biomedical staff in cultural competence
    • Hybrid spaces: Clinic incorporating ritual elements
    • Community health workers: Bicultural mediators

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of medical anthropology, illness/disease, explanatory models
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of symbolic analysis, structural violence, pluralism
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear cultural logic of healing, specific integration strategies
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to medical anthropology, colonial context
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from cultural meaning to practical application

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 3: Digital Ethnography of Online Gaming Community¶

    Event Description: Massively multiplayer online game (MMO) develops complex in-game society over 5 years. Player ethnography reveals: elaborate economy with virtual currency tradeable for real money, guilds with formal hierarchies and constitutions, dispute resolution mechanisms, initiation rituals for new members, shared mythology and origin stories, distinct linguistic register and slang, gift economies alongside market transactions, strong in-group identity ("gamers" vs. "normies"), moral codes around fair play and cheating. Guild leaders command respect comparable to offline authority figures. Players spend 20-40 hours weekly in-game, forming meaningful relationships and deriving identity from virtual accomplishments.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the virtual community through anthropological frameworks.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Culture and Symbol Systems (Geertz):
    • Culture as webs of significance: Game as meaningful symbolic universe
    • Thick description: Layers of meaning in game actions (not just "play")
    • Ritual and myth: Origin stories, initiation, shared narratives
    • Indigenous semiotics: Community creates its own meaning systems

    • Virtual ethnography: Culture exists in non-physical spaces

    • Social Organization and Political Anthropology:

    • Guilds as political units: Hierarchy, governance, constitutions
    • Segmentary lineage system analog: Nested alliances
    • Big Man leadership vs. formal authority (Melanesian parallels)
    • Dispute resolution: Informal law and social control

    • Emergent institutions without state apparatus

    • Economic Anthropology: Reciprocity and Exchange:

    • Gift economy (Mauss): Reciprocal obligations create bonds
    • Market economy: Virtual goods commodified
    • Hybrid systems: Gift and commodity logics coexist
    • Real money trading: Virtual/real boundary blurring

    • Spheres of exchange: Different goods circulate differently (Bohannan)

    • Ritual and Rites of Passage (Van Gennep, Turner):

    • Initiation rituals: New member incorporation
    • Liminality: Transitional states (leveling up, raids)
    • Communitas: Anti-structural bonding during shared challenges
    • Rituals create group solidarity and mark status transitions

    • Performance and efficacy: Rituals meaningful even if "virtual"

    • Identity and Community (Benedict Anderson, Gellner):

    • Imagined community: Members never meet but feel connected
    • Group identity: "Gamers" as cultural category
    • Stigma management (Goffman): "Normies" as out-group
    • Virtual identity vs. "real" identity dichotomy problematized

    • Authenticity: Online relationships as genuine social bonds

    • Language and Linguistic Anthropology:

    • Linguistic register: In-group specialized vocabulary
    • Slang and code-switching: Boundary marking
    • Sapir-Whorf: Language shapes game reality perception
    • Naming practices: Avatars, character names as identity

    • Communicative competence: Knowing how to speak as gamer

    • Play, Ritual, and Homo Ludens (Huizinga):

    • Play as fundamental human activity
    • Magic circle: Bounded space/time of play (but permeable)
    • Play creates culture: Rules, meaning, social order
    • "Just a game" minimizes meaningful social phenomena

    • Blurring: Play/work, virtual/real dichotomies collapse

    • Digital Anthropology and Method:

    • Participant observation in virtual spaces
    • Digital natives and culture: No less "real" than offline
    • Ethnographic challenges: Anonymity, access, ethics
    • Cyborg anthropology: Human-technology entanglement
    • Post-Durkheimian: Culture not tied to physical place

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of symbolic anthropology, exchange, ritual theory
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of cultural analysis, identity, social organization
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear identification of cultural patterns, parallels to offline societies
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to classic anthropology, digital anthropology
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from observation to cultural interpretation

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 4: Food Taboos and Cultural Meaning Systems¶

    Event Description: Comparative analysis of food taboos across cultures: Hindu beef avoidance (cow as sacred), Jewish and Muslim pork prohibition, Jain extreme vegetarianism (ahimsa), European horse meat taboo, East Asian dog meat controversies, American disgust at insects despite nutritional value. Material explanations (ecological, health) fail to fully explain patterns: Pigs would be efficient protein in Middle East, cows expensive to maintain in India with limited grazing. Taboos persist despite nutritional costs. Violation provokes moral outrage, not just preference. Food taboos mark group boundaries, and immigrants pressured to maintain practices.

    Analysis Task: Analyze food taboos through anthropological theories of culture and meaning.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Symbolic and Structuralist Approaches (Douglas, Levi-Strauss):
    • Food as symbol system, not just nutrition
    • Purity and Danger (Douglas): Taboos police symbolic boundaries
    • Anomalous animals: Pigs (neither fully land nor fully hoof-split), violate categories
    • Structural oppositions: Sacred/profane, clean/unclean, edible/inedible

    • Totem and taboo: Animals as good to think with (Levi-Strauss)

    • Cultural Materialism Critique (Harris):

    • Materialist explanation: Pork prohibition in hot climates (trichinosis, resource inefficiency)
    • Cow protection in India: More valuable alive (traction, dung fuel, milk) than dead
    • Ecological adaptation: Taboos as resource management
    • BUT: Symbolic dimension not reducible to material base

    • Overdetermination: Material and symbolic factors both operative

    • Durkheim: Sacred and Profane:

    • Sacred cows: Totem representing collective values
    • Totemism: Animal identifies group, forbidden to kill/eat
    • Collective representation: Cow = Hindu identity
    • Ritual regulation: Taboo maintains boundary between sacred and profane

    • Social function: Strengthens group solidarity through shared restrictions

    • Group Identity and Boundary Maintenance (Barth):

    • Food taboos as ethnic markers: "We don't eat X"
    • Boundary work: Distinguish in-group from out-group
    • Commensality: Eating together creates community, taboos restrict who you eat with
    • Dietary laws maintain diaspora identity (Jewish kashrut)

    • Immigrants resist host food culture to preserve identity

    • Moral Disgust and Ethical Systems:

    • Disgust as moral emotion (not just visceral)
    • Animals as quasi-persons: Dogs as companions, not food (in West)
    • Jain ahimsa: Non-violence extended to all life
    • Western vegetarianism: Animal rights, environmental ethics

    • Cultural relativism: No universal standard of edible/inedible

    • Power, Hegemony, and Resistance:

    • Dominant groups impose food standards: Colonizers stigmatize indigenous diets
    • Dog meat controversy: Western cultural imperialism vs. cultural relativism
    • Insect consumption potential: Overcoming Western bias
    • Globalization: McDonald's vs. local food practices

    • Food as resistance: Maintaining traditional diet asserts autonomy

    • Anthropology of Taste (Bourdieu, Mintz):

    • Taste is cultural, not natural: Acquired through socialization
    • Habitus: Embodied dispositions (disgust is learned)
    • Distinction: Food choices signal class, education, identity
    • Historical construction: Horse meat taboo post-19th century in US

    • Sweetness and Power (Mintz): Food meanings tied to political economy

    • Applied Implications:

    • Nutrition interventions must respect cultural meaning
    • Insect protein adoption requires cultural reframing
    • Food security: Some solutions culturally unacceptable
    • Multicultural contexts: Accommodate diverse dietary practices
    • No universal food rationality

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of Douglas, Durkheim, structuralism, materialism
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of symbolic analysis, boundary work, power dynamics
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear explanation of specific taboos through cultural logic
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to anthropological theory, comparative cases
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from observation to symbolic interpretation

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 5: Revitalization Movement and Cultural Change¶

    Event Description: Indigenous community experiences rapid cultural change due to resource extraction on traditional lands: young people migrate to cities for work, traditional language declining (only elders fluent), ceremonial practices eroding, social problems increase (substance abuse, suicide). Charismatic leader emerges proposing cultural revitalization: language immersion schools, revival of banned ceremonies, land reclamation activism, rejection of Western influences (alcohol, Christianity), return to "traditional ways." Movement attracts youth, creates tension between revivalists and assimilationists. Government sees movement as threat; some anthropologists critique as "invented tradition." Movement provides purpose and identity for demoralized community.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the revitalization movement through anthropological frameworks of culture change.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Revitalization Movements (Wallace):
    • Response to cultural stress and disintegration
    • Stages: Steady state Stress Revitalization New steady state (or failure)
    • Prophetic leadership: Charismatic figure with vision
    • Nativistic movements: Rejection of foreign elements

    • Mazeway resynthesis: New cultural integration

    • Acculturation and Culture Change (Herskovits, Redfield):

    • Forced acculturation: Colonialism, missionization, resource extraction
    • Cultural disintegration: Loss of coherence, anomie
    • Syncretism vs. compartmentalization: Blending vs. separation
    • Deculturation: Loss of original culture without full adoption of new

    • Resistance movements: Agency in face of domination

    • Ghost Dance and Cargo Cult Parallels:

    • Ghost Dance (1890): Lakota response to conquest, promise of White departure and buffalo return
    • Cargo Cults (Melanesia): Ritual practices to obtain Western goods through spiritual means
    • Millenarian aspects: Apocalyptic transformation, restoration of past golden age
    • Rational response to irrational situation (Worsley)

    • Combining traditional and introduced elements

    • Invented Tradition (Hobsbawm):

    • Traditions represented as ancient may be recent constructions
    • Critique: "Traditional ways" may be idealized, ahistorical
    • Counter-critique: All traditions invented at some point
    • Strategic essentialism (Spivak): Political utility of unified identity

    • Authenticity debates: Who defines "real" tradition?

    • Collective Identity and Resistance (Scott):

    • Hidden transcripts: Public compliance, private resistance
    • Revitalization as public transcript of resistance
    • Identity politics: Culture as basis for political mobilization
    • Fourth World indigenous movements: Global pattern

    • Culture as resource: Strategic deployment in land claims

    • Structural Violence and Historical Trauma (Farmer, Brave Heart):

    • Colonial legacy: Land theft, cultural genocide, residential schools
    • Intergenerational trauma: Psychological wounds transmitted
    • Social suffering: Not individual pathology but structural violence
    • Substance abuse and suicide as symptoms of cultural disintegration

    • Healing requires cultural restoration, not just individual treatment

    • Agency and Cultural Creativity (Sahlins):

    • Not passive victims: Indigenous people as active agents
    • Structure and agency dialectic: Constraint and creativity
    • Culture is dynamic, not static museum piece
    • Selective adoption: Take useful elements, reject harmful

    • Indigeneity as mode of political action (Niezen)

    • Applied Anthropology and Ethics:

    • Anthropologist role: Advocate, ally, or neutral observer?
    • Cultural survival: Support self-determination
    • Critique of romanticization: Avoid noble savage trope
    • Decolonizing anthropology: Indigenous-led research
    • Practical solidarity: Support language programs, land rights

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of revitalization theory, acculturation, invented tradition
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of historical context, structural violence, agency
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear explanation of movement dynamics, comparative parallels
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to Ghost Dance, cargo cults, indigenous movements
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from cultural stress to revitalization response

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Passing Criteria¶

    To demonstrate anthropologist analyst competence:

    • Minimum per scenario: 35/50 (70%)
    • Overall minimum: 175/250 (70%)
    • Must pass at least 4 of 5 scenarios

    Evaluation Dimensions¶

    Each scenario is scored on:

    1. Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of anthropological theories and concepts
    2. Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of cultural analysis and interpretation
    3. Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear identification of cultural patterns and meanings
    4. Historical Grounding (0-10): Use of classic and contemporary anthropological theory
    5. Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from observation to cultural interpretation

    What High-Quality Analysis Looks Like¶

    Excellent (45-50 points):

    • Applies multiple relevant anthropological theories accurately
    • Provides thick description and cultural interpretation
    • Makes specific predictions about cultural dynamics and meanings
    • Cites classic anthropologists and contemporary ethnography
    • Clear logical flow from observation to cultural pattern to theory
    • Acknowledges cultural complexity, avoids essentialization
    • Identifies non-obvious symbolic meanings and structural relationships

    Good (35-44 points):

    • Applies key anthropological frameworks correctly
    • Considers cultural context and meaning systems
    • Makes reasonable cultural interpretations
    • References relevant theory and ethnographic examples
    • Clear reasoning
    • Provides useful anthropological insights

    Needs Improvement (<35 points):

    • Misapplies anthropological theory or uses only common sense
    • Ignores cultural meaning and symbolic dimensions
    • Ethnocentric judgments rather than cultural relativism
    • Lacks theoretical grounding or ethnographic evidence
    • Unclear or illogical reasoning
    • Superficial cultural analysis

    Using This Quiz¶

    For Self-Assessment¶

    1. Attempt each scenario analysis
    2. Compare your analysis to expected elements
    3. Score yourself honestly on each dimension
    4. Identify areas for improvement

    For Automated Testing (Claude Agent SDK)¶

    from claude_agent_sdk import Agent, TestHarness

agent = Agent.load("anthropologist-analyst")
quiz = load_quiz_scenarios("tests/quiz.md")

results = []
for scenario in quiz.scenarios:
    analysis = agent.analyze(scenario.event)
    score = evaluate_analysis(analysis, scenario.expected_elements)
    results.append({"scenario": scenario.name, "score": score})

assert sum(r["score"] for r in results) >= 175  # Overall passing
assert sum(1 for r in results if r["score"] >= 35) >= 4  # At least 4 scenarios pass

    For Continuous Improvement¶

    • Add new scenarios as cultural phenomena evolve
    • Update expected elements as anthropological theory advances
    • Refine scoring criteria based on analysis quality patterns
    • Use failures to improve anthropologist analyst skill
