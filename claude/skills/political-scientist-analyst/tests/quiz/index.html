    Political Scientist Analyst - Domain Validation Quiz¶

    Purpose¶

    This quiz validates that the political scientist analyst applies political science frameworks correctly, identifies power dynamics and institutional factors, and provides well-grounded analysis. Each scenario requires demonstration of political reasoning, framework application, and evidence-based conclusions.

    Scenario 1: Coalition Government Formation After Hung Parliament¶

    Event Description: A national election produces a hung parliament with no party holding a majority. Results: Center-left party 35%, center-right party 32%, green party 15%, populist party 12%, regional party 6%. The center-left party (largest) has 14 days to form a coalition government or the center-right party gets its turn. Historical context: This country uses proportional representation, coalition governments are common, and policy distance between parties varies significantly. The green party is policy-compatible with center-left but demands cabinet positions and climate policy concessions. The populist party offers support but demands immigration restrictions that alienate the greens. The regional party demands autonomy provisions.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the coalition formation dynamics and predict the most likely outcome.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Coalition Theory Application:
    • Minimum winning coalition (smallest majority possible to maximize payoffs)
    • Ideologically connected coalitions (policy distance matters)
    • Formateur advantage (largest party has first-mover advantage)

    • Office-seeking vs. policy-seeking motivations

    • Institutional Constraints:

    • 14-day formation deadline creates time pressure
    • Proportional representation system affects bargaining
    • Cabinet formation rules and norms

    • Investiture vote requirements

    • Power Dynamics:

    • Pivotal party analysis (which party is kingmaker?)
    • Coalition arithmetic: Multiple viable combinations (35+15=50%, 35+12=47%, 32+15+6=53%)
    • Bargaining power: Green party is pivotal for center-left

    • Outside options and BATNA (Best Alternative To Negotiated Agreement)

    • Policy Space Analysis:

    • Spatial modeling: Policy positions on economic and social dimensions
    • Center-left + Green = ideologically connected, minimal policy distance
    • Center-left + Populist = ideologically distant, high transaction costs

    • Grand coalition (center-left + center-right) = centrist but wastes seats

    • Strategic Considerations:

    • Future electoral consequences of coalition choices
    • Blame avoidance vs. credit claiming
    • Coalition stability and durability

    • Commitment problems and credible promises

    • Historical Precedents:

    • Similar coalition formations in this country
    • Comparative cases (Germany, Netherlands, Belgium)
    • Durability patterns of different coalition types
    • Lessons from minority government attempts

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of coalition theory, spatial models, institutional analysis
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of power dynamics, policy space, strategic analysis
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear prediction with probabilistic reasoning
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to comparative cases, precedents
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow from theory to evidence to predictions

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 2: Democratic Backsliding in Hybrid Regime¶

    Event Description: A democratically elected government in a hybrid regime (semi-democratic, semi-authoritarian) begins systematic erosion of democratic institutions over 3 years. Actions include: stacking the constitutional court with loyalists, criminalizing opposition media as "foreign agents," gerrymandering electoral districts, restricting NGO activities, weakening legislative oversight, and using state resources for partisan advantage. The government maintains 55% approval ratings due to strong economic growth and nationalist rhetoric. International observers downgrade the country's democracy rating from "free" to "partly free." Opposition parties are fragmented, civil society is weak, and the military remains neutral but loyal to the constitution.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the democratic backsliding dynamics and assess the regime's trajectory.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Democratic Backsliding Framework:
    • Levitsky & Ziblatt indicators: Rejection of democratic norms, denial of legitimacy to opponents, toleration of violence, curtailing civil liberties
    • Stealth authoritarianism: Legalistic but anti-democratic measures
    • Incremental vs. sudden regime change

    • Point of no return analysis

    • Institutional Analysis:

    • Horizontal accountability breakdown (courts, legislature compromised)
    • Vertical accountability stress (media restrictions, electoral manipulation)
    • Checks and balances erosion

    • Constitutional safeguards vs. majoritarian abuse

    • Political Economy:

    • Rentier state dynamics (if applicable)
    • Economic performance legitimacy (55% approval from growth)
    • Distributional coalitions supporting regime

    • Elite incentives and coordination

    • Civil Society and Opposition:

    • Opposition fragmentation enables executive dominance
    • Collective action problems for civil society
    • Preference falsification and spiral of silence

    • Tipping point dynamics for mass mobilization

    • International Dimension:

    • Democratic diffusion vs. authoritarian learning
    • International pressure (observers, sanctions) vs. sovereignty claims
    • Regional context and neighborhood effects

    • External support for regime vs. opposition

    • Comparative Cases:

    • Hungary (Orbán), Poland (PiS), Turkey (Erdoğan)
    • Venezuela (Chávez/Maduro transition)
    • Russia (Putin's consolidation)
    • Lessons: Trajectories, reversibility, stabilization points

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of backsliding theory, institutional analysis
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of dynamics, mechanisms, trajectories
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear assessment of regime trajectory and intervention points
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to comparative cases, empirical patterns
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical analysis of causal mechanisms

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 3: Social Movement Mobilization and Policy Change¶

    Event Description: A social movement emerges demanding police reform after a series of high-profile incidents of police violence. The movement employs diverse tactics: peaceful protests (hundreds of thousands in major cities), civil disobedience, social media campaigns (#ReformPolice trending), electoral mobilization (targeting incumbents), and legislative lobbying. Counter-mobilization occurs with pro-police groups. Public opinion shifts from 35% support for major reform (before incidents) to 58% support (after 6 months of activism). The ruling party faces internal divisions: progressive wing supports reform, moderate wing fears alienating police unions and suburban voters. Municipal governments begin implementing modest reforms, but national legislation stalls in parliament. Movement leaders debate whether to accept incremental change or hold out for comprehensive reform.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the movement's prospects for achieving policy change and provide strategic recommendations.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Social Movement Theory:
    • Resource mobilization: Organizational capacity, funding, networks
    • Political opportunity structure: Electoral vulnerability, elite divisions, public opinion shifts
    • Framing: Issue framing as justice vs. public safety, resonance with broader values

    • Tactical repertoire: Protests, civil disobedience, electoral, legislative strategies

    • Power and Influence:

    • Inside vs. outside strategies (lobbying vs. protest)
    • Disruption power vs. electoral threat
    • Coalition building with sympathetic elites

    • Media attention and agenda-setting

    • Political Context:

    • Electoral cycle (upcoming elections create pressure)
    • Partisan polarization (bipartisan support needed?)
    • Federalism: Municipal vs. state vs. national reform

    • Veto points in legislative process

    • Policy Change Dynamics:

    • Window of opportunity (crisis-driven attention)
    • Policy feedback effects (incremental reforms create constituencies)
    • Path dependence and institutional stickiness

    • Implementation challenges (bureaucratic resistance, police unions)

    • Strategic Dilemmas:

    • Radical flank effect: Do disruptive tactics help or hurt?
    • Incremental vs. comprehensive change trade-off
    • Institutionalization vs. grassroots energy

    • Coalition maintenance vs. ideological purity

    • Historical Precedents:

    • Civil rights movement (1960s US)
    • Anti-apartheid movement (South Africa)
    • Recent movements: Marriage equality, climate activism
    • Lessons: Successful paths to policy change, common failures

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of social movement theory, policy change frameworks
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of power dynamics, strategic analysis
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear strategic recommendations grounded in theory
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to comparative movements, empirical patterns
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical analysis of strategic trade-offs

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 4: Ethnic Conflict and Power-Sharing Negotiations¶

    Event Description: A multi-ethnic state with a history of inter-communal violence (last major episode 15 years ago) faces renewed tensions. Demographics: Majority Group A (55%), Minority Group B (30%), Minority Group C (15%). Triggering events: Group A-dominated government proposes citizenship law perceived as discriminatory by Group B; Group B leaders call for boycott and autonomy; Group C fears being caught in crossfire. Low-level violence erupts: riots, property destruction, 50 deaths. International mediators propose power-sharing agreement with provisions for: proportional representation in legislature, consociational executive (rotating presidency or grand coalition cabinet), minority veto on sensitive issues, territorial autonomy for Group B regions, and truth and reconciliation commission. Group A hardliners resist ("majority rule, why should we compromise?"), Group B demands federalism and explicit constitutional recognition, Group C wants guarantees of protection.

    Analysis Task: Analyze the conflict dynamics and assess the viability of the proposed power-sharing agreement.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Ethnic Conflict Theory:
    • Security dilemma in divided societies (information and commitment problems)
    • Ethnic outbidding and political entrepreneurs
    • Ancient hatreds vs. constructed identities debate

    • Triggering events and spiral of violence

    • Power-Sharing Analysis:

    • Consociational democracy (Lijphart): Grand coalition, proportionality, veto rights, autonomy
    • Critiques: Freezes ethnic identities, rewards extremism, rigid
    • Alternatives: Integrative institutions (incentivize moderation), partition

    • Commitment credibility: How to ensure durable agreement?

    • Institutional Design:

    • Electoral system: Proportional representation vs. majoritarian
    • Executive arrangements: Rotating presidency vs. consensus cabinet
    • Federal vs. unitary structure
    • Veto rights: Protection vs. paralysis trade-off

    • Judicial review and constitutional safeguards

    • Group Dynamics:

    • Intra-group divisions (moderates vs. hardliners within each group)
    • Group C as swing actor and potential mediator
    • Demographic futures and long-term viability

    • Changing identities and cross-cutting cleavages

    • International Dimension:

    • Mediator interests and leverage
    • Precedent effects (other conflicts watching)
    • Enforcement mechanisms (peacekeepers, sanctions)

    • Regional spillover risks

    • Historical Precedents:

    • Northern Ireland (Good Friday Agreement)
    • Bosnia (Dayton Accords)
    • Lebanon (consociational system)
    • South Africa (negotiated transition)
    • Lessons: Success factors, failure patterns, durability

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of ethnic conflict theory, power-sharing analysis
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of institutional design, group dynamics, viability assessment
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear assessment of agreement viability and specific recommendations
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to comparative cases, lessons learned
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical analysis of trade-offs and commitment problems

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Scenario 5: Populist Party Electoral Breakthrough and Governing Challenge¶

    Event Description: A populist outsider party wins 38% in national elections, forming a minority government with confidence-and-supply support from a smaller party. The populist party's platform: anti-establishment rhetoric, nationalism, protectionism, skepticism of international institutions, direct democracy proposals (frequent referendums), and anti-corruption crusade. Leadership style: charismatic leader with social media prowess, attacks on "elite media," and confrontational approach to institutions. Governing challenges emerge: Bureaucracy resists policy implementation; constitutional court blocks several initiatives as unconstitutional; diplomatic tensions with allies over foreign policy; economic uncertainty from protectionist policies; media scrutiny intensifies; approval ratings decline from 45% (honeymoon) to 35% (6 months in). The party faces strategic choices: Moderate and institutionalize (risk base alienation) or double down on populist rhetoric (risk governance failure and institutional crisis).

    Analysis Task: Analyze the populist party's governing dilemma and predict its likely trajectory.

    Expected Analysis Elements¶

    • Populism Theory:
    • Thin ideology: People vs. elite, anti-pluralism, majoritarianism
    • Discourse analysis: Enemy construction, simplification, emotional appeals
    • Left vs. right populism (economic dimension)

    • Populism in power vs. opposition (different dynamics)

    • Governing Challenges:

    • Outsider expertise deficit (lack of policy competence)
    • Institutional friction (bureaucracy, courts, media)
    • Coalition management with pragmatic support party

    • Policy delivery vs. symbolic politics trade-off

    • Strategic Dilemmas:

    • Moderation: Gain governing effectiveness, lose base support, become establishment
    • Radicalization: Maintain base, provoke institutional crisis, risk collapse
    • Middle path: Selective battles, strategic retreats, difficult to sustain

    • Electoral incentives vs. governance incentives

    • Institutional Responses:

    • Democratic guardrails: Courts, media, civil society
    • Establishment adaptation (co-optation vs. resistance)
    • Opposition strategies (normalize vs. isolate)

    • Constitutional resilience vs. vulnerability

    • Political Economy:

    • Economic performance impact on support
    • Distributional coalitions (who benefits from policies?)
    • Protectionism consequences (short-term gains, long-term costs)

    • Competence signaling through economic management

    • Comparative Cases:

    • Italy (Five Star Movement governing experience)
    • Greece (Syriza confrontation with EU)
    • Austria (Freedom Party in coalition)
    • Latin American populism (Chávez, Kirchner, López Obrador)
    • Lessons: Successful institutionalization, failures, trajectories

    Evaluation Criteria¶

    • Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of populism theory, institutional analysis
    • Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness of governing dilemmas, strategic trade-offs
    • Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear prediction of trajectory with causal reasoning
    • Historical Grounding (0-10): References to comparative cases, empirical patterns
    • Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical analysis of strategic choices and consequences

    Minimum Passing Score: 35/50

    Passing Criteria¶

    To demonstrate political scientist analyst competence:

    • Minimum per scenario: 35/50 (70%)
    • Overall minimum: 175/250 (70%)
    • Must pass at least 4 of 5 scenarios

    Evaluation Dimensions¶

    Each scenario is scored on:

    1. Domain Accuracy (0-10): Correct application of political science frameworks
    2. Analytical Depth (0-10): Thoroughness and sophistication
    3. Insight Specificity (0-10): Clear, actionable insights and predictions
    4. Historical Grounding (0-10): Use of evidence, precedents, comparative cases
    5. Reasoning Clarity (0-10): Logical flow, coherent argument

    What High-Quality Analysis Looks Like¶

    Excellent (45-50 points):

    • Applies multiple relevant frameworks accurately
    • Considers competing perspectives and mechanisms
    • Makes specific, probabilistic predictions with causal reasoning
    • Cites comparative cases and empirical patterns
    • Clear logical flow from theory to evidence to conclusions
    • Acknowledges uncertainties and scope conditions
    • Identifies non-obvious strategic dynamics

    Good (35-44 points):

    • Applies key frameworks correctly
    • Considers main mechanisms and trade-offs
    • Makes reasonable predictions with justification
    • References some comparative cases or precedents
    • Clear reasoning
    • Provides useful insights

    Needs Improvement (<35 points):

    • Misapplies political science frameworks
    • Ignores important dynamics or mechanisms
    • Vague or incorrect predictions
    • Lacks empirical grounding
    • Unclear or illogical reasoning
    • Superficial analysis

    Using This Quiz¶

    For Self-Assessment¶

    1. Attempt each scenario analysis
    2. Compare your analysis to expected elements
    3. Score yourself honestly on each dimension
    4. Identify areas for improvement

    For Automated Testing (Claude Agent SDK)¶

    from claude_agent_sdk import Agent, TestHarness

agent = Agent.load("political-scientist-analyst")
quiz = load_quiz_scenarios("tests/quiz.md")

results = []
for scenario in quiz.scenarios:
    analysis = agent.analyze(scenario.event)
    score = evaluate_analysis(analysis, scenario.expected_elements)
    results.append({"scenario": scenario.name, "score": score})

assert sum(r["score"] for r in results) >= 175  # Overall passing
assert sum(1 for r in results if r["score"] >= 35) >= 4  # At least 4 scenarios pass

    For Continuous Improvement¶

    • Add new scenarios as political events unfold
    • Update expected elements as political science research evolves
    • Refine scoring criteria based on analysis quality patterns
    • Use failures to improve political scientist analyst skill
